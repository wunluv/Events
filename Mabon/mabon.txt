## San's writeup on the market to the private Vendor Whatsapp Group

Hey friends! Lovely to see the aliveness in this co-created gathering. Thanks to Stella for the exquisite coms and coordination here. Big thanks to all of you for your willingness to play and support.

I wanted to share some context and thinking behind the event:

In the global south we move into the embrace of Autumn and the second harvest. This is the season of thanksgiving, as the Earths tilt moves us away from the sun, we reflect on all that was realised in the last season. We set intentions for the next quarter.

With the hum drum of life, we tend to overlook all that we have accomplished and we get entangled in the undone todo list. More often than not we forget to acknowledge what we have accomplished in partnership with the land and the people we work with.

Our intention with this event is to initiate a celebration of the seasonal cycles of the solstices and equinoxes with community. This brings more intention to how we navigate the cycles, by honouring our good work and giving thanks to the land. Core themes are:
- connection and awareness with our natural world and the cycles
- our community
- our land
- celebrating local
- a better relationship with our food and where it comes from
- gratitude, intention and discernment

We want to foster a better understanding of cosmic cycles.

For example, we in the south follow the north's lead by celebrating Easter (Ostara: spring, new growth and fertility) during our harvest time! We celebrate Halloween (last harvest before winter, the cornucopia, abundance, thanksgiving, entering the underworld, death, contemplation etc.) in October, which is actually our spring! This makes no sense.

Most of my farming activity is guided strongly by the cosmic cycles. My inner clock knows to plant seeds during an ascending moon for example, and my lifestyle naturally syncs with that lunar rhythm. I know that when the autumn leaves have almost all fallen, I can tend to pruning fruit trees and blueberry bushes. As a result the mental clutter reduces, my nervous system relaxes and I find a harmony within myself and with nature.

This smol catalyst market is our attempt to bring this awareness to more people so that more of us can sync our rhythms that ultimate benefit all of us and our land.

I'm long on local thriving and resilient food growing; and more importantly on the community that are engaged in this. Ultimately this startup event (and our intentional community Beeja Mara) are designed around these core values.

Here's to more harmony,
San

## Information extracted from the internet
Mabon - September 23, 2019
Mabon is a pagan holiday, and one of the eight Wiccan sabbats celebrated during the year. Mabon celebrates the autumnal equinox. In the northern hemisphere, this September 23rd will be the autumnal equinox. However, the southern hemisphere already celebrated Mabon on March 20, when the Northern hemisphere celebrated Ostara. It also celebrates the mid-harvest festival (also known as the second harvest).

Many civilizations have celebrated a harvest festival around the equinox. In the 1700s, the Bavarians (part of present day Germany) began a festival that starts in the last week of September. They called this festival Oktoberfest. The festival had lots of feasting and celebrating. Oktoberfest is still celebrated in Bavaria today.


Photo of a Mabon altar. courtesy of Flickr user Colleen. Available at https://www.flickr.com/photos/pearlshelf/8726509704
Many cultures see the second harvest (after the first harvest Lammas) and equinox as a time for giving thanks. This time of year is when farmers know how well their summer crops did, and how well fed their animals have become. This determines whether you and your family would have enough food for the winter. That is why people used to give thanks around this time, thanks for their crops, and animals, and food. The original American Thanksgiving was celebrated on October 3, which makes more sense with harvest times. By the end of November, there’s not that much left to harvest.

The name Mabon comes from the Welsh God, who was the son of the Earth Mother Goddess. However, there is evidence that the name was adopted in the 1970s, and the holiday was not originally a Celtic celebration.

To celebrate this holiday, pagans might pick apples. Apples are a common symbol of the second harvest. They may use the apples in an apple harvest ritual that thanks the gods for the bountiful harvest. Others might perform a ritual to restore balance and harmony to their lives, as this holiday celebrates a day with equal light and day. Another common ritual is to set up an altar with symbols of the season, such as apples, grapes, and other seasonal harvests. Any sabbat would not be complete without a feast for family and friends.
